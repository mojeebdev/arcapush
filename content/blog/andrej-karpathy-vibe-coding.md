## One Post That Changed Software

On February 2, 2025, Andrej Karpathy posted on X:

> "There's a new kind of coding I call 'vibe coding', where you fully give in to the vibes, embrace exponentials, and forget that the code even exists."

Within 48 hours the post had been shared hundreds of thousands of times. Within weeks, every major technology publication had covered it. By the end of 2025, Collins English Dictionary had named **vibe coding** their Word of the Year.

That is an unusually fast journey from tweet to cultural moment. Understanding why requires understanding who Andrej Karpathy is and what he was actually describing.

---

## Who is Andrej Karpathy

Andrej Karpathy is one of the most influential figures in modern AI. He was a founding member of OpenAI, served as the Director of AI at Tesla where he led the development of Autopilot, and returned to OpenAI before eventually leaving to work on independent AI education projects.

His technical credentials are exceptional — a Stanford PhD, foundational research in deep learning, and a track record of building AI systems at scale. When Karpathy says something about how AI is changing software development, the technical community pays attention.

That credibility is part of why the vibe coding post landed so hard. This was not a marketing claim from a tool company. It was an observation from someone who had spent years building AI systems, describing a genuine shift in how software gets made.

---

## What Karpathy Actually Said

The full post described a workflow where Karpathy was using AI tools — specifically voice-to-code interfaces — to build software by describing what he wanted rather than writing code manually.

The key insight was not just that AI could generate code. It was that the relationship between the builder and the code had fundamentally changed. In traditional development, the developer is responsible for every line. In vibe coding, the developer describes intent and the AI handles implementation.

Karpathy described the experience as almost meditative — following the flow of building without the friction of syntax, debugging, and implementation details. Hence: **vibes**.

He was also honest about the limitations. AI-generated code is not always correct. Security can be an issue. The approach works best when the builder understands what they are trying to achieve, even if they cannot write every line themselves.

---

## Why the Term Went Viral

Three things made "vibe coding" stick where other terms had not.

**It was accurate.** Anyone who had spent time building with AI tools immediately recognized what Karpathy was describing. The term gave language to an experience that was already happening but had no name.

**It was memorable.** "Vibe coding" is evocative in a way that "AI-assisted development" or "prompt-driven programming" never could be. It captures the feeling of the workflow, not just the mechanics.

**The source was credible.** A term coined by a founder of OpenAI carries weight that the same term from a product marketer would not.

---

## The Ecosystem That Followed

In the twelve months after Karpathy's post, the vibe coding ecosystem expanded dramatically:

**Lovable** raised at a $6.6B valuation, backed by Alphabet's CapitalG, Nvidia, and Accel — positioning itself as the definitive full-stack vibe coding tool for non-technical founders.

**Cursor** reached a $29.3B valuation, backed by Andreessen Horowitz and Accel — becoming the dominant tool for technical builders who wanted AI deeply integrated into their existing workflow.

**Replit** continued to grow its AI-native development environment, supported by Google and Andreessen Horowitz.

Dozens of smaller companies entered the space. The term vibe coding began appearing in job descriptions, pitch decks, and venture fund theses.

---

## Collins Word of the Year 2025

Collins English Dictionary selecting vibe coding as their Word of the Year for 2025 was the moment the concept crossed from tech culture into general culture.

Collins defines vibe coding as: *the practice of using artificial intelligence to write computer code by describing in natural language what one wants the program to do.*

The selection was notable because Collins typically chooses words that reflect broader cultural shifts, not just industry trends. Vibe coding made the list because it captured something genuinely new about how millions of people were beginning to relate to technology and creation.

---

## What Karpathy Started

Karpathy did not invent the tools that make vibe coding possible. AI code generation existed before his post. What he did was name a practice, articulate its implications, and — by virtue of who he is — give it legitimacy at exactly the moment it needed it.

The vibe coding ecosystem that followed is now worth hundreds of billions of dollars in combined valuation. The community of vibe coders building real products is measured in the millions.

And it started with one post on X.
